P04 — OBJECTIONS DOJO (MODE 4 of 5)
====================================

Prerequisites: P01, P02, P03.

GOAL OF THIS MODE

The Guide's Chapter 14 lists seven common objections to the book
and states the book's response to each. This mode renders those
objection/response pairs as a DIALECTIC — not a FAQ, not a quiz,
not a defense monologue. The user selects an objection they find
compelling; the book's response is presented; the user is asked
what they would say in reply; and a final panel shows where the
book's argument is willing to stop short of converting the reader.

The central educational move is to demonstrate, in interaction,
what a serious philosophical dialectic looks like: each side
committed, each side responsive, neither side pretending to have
dissolved the other's best objection through rhetoric. A user
leaves the mode equipped with (a) a map of where the book is
strong, (b) a map of where the book concedes it cannot persuade
without a shared premise, and (c) a felt sense of the book's
posture toward its critics.


----------------------------------------------------------------------
PART A — THE SEVEN CANONICAL OBJECTIONS
----------------------------------------------------------------------

From Guide Chapter 14, in canonical order:

  14.1 "This Framework Has No Empirical Evidence."
       Target: CRI, PCT. The objection: the book reasons
       theoretically and does not submit its claims to empirical
       test. Book's response (14.1): data stand inside arguments,
       not outside them; empirical investigation is one method of
       searching for coherence; a reader rejecting the
       coherence-at-limit view will find the absence of citation
       apparatus a feature, not a bug. The response REFRAMES; it
       does not REFUTE.

  14.2 "Your Argument for Anarchism Assumes Benevolent Human
        Nature."
       Target: MCS. The objection: state abolition seems to
       require that freed people behave decently, while the book
       elsewhere denies this. Book's response (14.2): the argument
       is STRUCTURAL, not psychological. Voluntary coordination is
       more coherent than coerced coordination; residual
       irrationality persists and is better managed by market,
       reputational, and argumentative mechanisms than by
       coercion.

  14.3 "Marx Was Right About Silent Compulsion, So Capitalism Is
        Coercive."
       Target: MCS. The objection: silent compulsion coerces in
       substance even if not in form. Book's response (14.3):
       accepts the first half; rejects the second. Silent
       compulsion is real; it is categorically distinct from
       state coercion because it does not prohibit exit. The
       golden-chain / iron-chain distinction.

  14.4 "Your Profit-Coherence Thesis Is Unfalsifiable."
       Target: PCT. The objection: when a firm is profitable, the
       book calls its premises coherent; when unprofitable,
       incoherent; the thesis does no work. Book's response
       (14.4): the thesis is falsifiable in principle by
       demonstration of internal contradiction OR by an empirical
       case where coherence-superior premises co-occur with less
       profit (time lag and distortion aside). Acknowledges
       absence of a coherence meter as an open problem, compares
       to pre-metric Newtonian mass.

  14.5 "This Framework Lets You Rationalize Anything."
       Target: DIAM. The objection: the framework can generate
       coherent-sounding accounts of any preferred conclusion.
       Book's response (14.5): the risk is real and applies to
       every philosophical system. Discipline — the Diamond
       Method's iterative self-correction — is the mitigation.
       Offers the book's own surprising conclusions (insider
       trading reading, Marx-as-diagnostician, state abolition)
       as evidence that the framework can be disciplined.

  14.6 "Your Case Studies Are Cherry-Picked."
       Target: PCT, PEF. The objection: the book's specific
       examples (iPhone, OpenAI vs. Anthropic, Palantir, Sears,
       De Beers) fit the thesis too well. Book's response (14.6):
       examples are illustrations, never proofs. A critic must
       refute the theoretical argument; producing
       counter-examples is insufficient, because any counter-
       example can be re-described and absorbed. Empirical readers
       will find this unsatisfactory; the book is honest about
       that.

  14.7 "The Book Is Politically Extreme."
       Target: the book as a whole. The objection: anarcho-
       capitalism is a marginal position; the book advocates
       extremism. Book's response (14.7): political marginality
       is not an argument. If the arguments are unsound, locate
       the unsoundness; if sound, their popularity is irrelevant.


----------------------------------------------------------------------
PART B — WHAT THE USER DOES
----------------------------------------------------------------------

B1. Entry.

Mode opens on the ObjectionDeck: seven cards laid out as a 3-2-2
arrangement (WIDE), 2-2-2-1 (STANDARD), or single-column
(NARROW). Each card shows:
    - Objection number and short title.
    - The objection text (1-2 sentences, authored paraphrase).
    - A "target" tag set (which claim(s) it targets), using the
      canonical claim IDs from P01.
    - An "urgency" rating (1-5). The urgency is NOT how correct
      the objection is; it is the book's own assessment of how
      often it is the first objection raised. 14.1 and 14.7 are
      rated 5; 14.4 is rated 4; others lower.

Header: "Seven objections the book expects. Which one is yours?"

Below the deck, a small persistent strip called the
MethodologyAdmission: a single line of copy, --fg-dim, mono:

    "The book answers on its own methodological terms. Some
     objections the book REFRAMES rather than REFUTES. See the
     last tile of each turn."

This line is not decorative; see Part C4.

B2. The turn.

The user picks an objection card. A DialecticTurn panel opens.
The turn has three "tiles", shown in order:

    TILE A. OBJECTION (full)
        The authored objection in full. 2-4 sentences. Serif —
        apply class .prp-prose (NO drop cap) from the utility
        defined in P01 §A4. Do not re-define typography here.

    TILE B. BOOK'S RESPONSE
        The authored response. 3-6 sentences. Serif. Apply class
        .prp-prose AND .prp-prose--drop-cap from the P01 §A4
        utility: this is the canonical drop-cap site in the
        suite, per the enumeration in P01 §A4 "Where this
        utility is applied". (If the authored response falls
        below ~120 words the drop cap still reads as intentional
        here because the response IS the pedagogical climax of
        the mode; unlike quadrant diagnoses where the drop cap
        would be ornamental.) Includes a clearly labeled
        sub-section called REFRAMES or REFUTES: a single word
        indicator. This indicator is the book's own
        self-description of its move, and it IS load-bearing.
        (14.1 and 14.7 are REFRAMES; 14.2 and 14.3 are REFUTES;
        14.4 is REFUTES-IN-PRINCIPLE; 14.5 is CONCEDES-AND-
        DISCIPLINES; 14.6 is REFRAMES.)

    TILE C. WHAT WOULD YOU SAY NEXT?
        The user is offered three authored continuation
        responses (radio) PLUS a free-text option. The three
        authored continuations represent:
            - A reader who accepts the book's response and
              wants to press further on an adjacent question.
            - A reader who is unconvinced and names the
              specific premise they reject.
            - A reader who is dismissive ("this is
              word-games").
        The user selects one or writes their own. Selection is
        recorded. There is no judgment of the choice.

After TILE C, a final summary strip:

    "The book's response here is a [REFRAME / REFUTATION /
     CONCESSION + DISCIPLINE]. If you still reject the response,
     the disagreement is likely at [CITED CLAIM ID], which you
     can open in the Ontology."

For REFRAMES specifically, the strip adds:

    "The book does not expect to convert a reader who rejects
     [CLAIM ID]. It asks only that the reader state an
     alternative account that does not reduce to [ALT]."

B3. Return to deck.

From a completed turn, the user may:
    - Return to the deck. Completed objections are marked
      "read" (mono check, --fg-dim). Their card may still be
      reopened; re-reading is normal.
    - Rank objections. After reading at least three, a "rank
      by personal weight" affordance appears on the deck: the
      user drags objections into a top-to-bottom list of which
      they find most compelling. This ranking is stored in the
      session but never transmitted. Its purpose is PERSONAL:
      the user learns, by ordering, where their own critical
      priority lies.
    - Open adjacent material: each turn has an "OPEN IN"
      affordance that can route to Ontology (on the cited
      claim) or to Diamond Method Practicum (if the case is
      treated there — e.g., 14.3 is treated in Case 1 of P03,
      "silent compulsion").

C2. src/lib/prp/objections.ts

    export type ResponseKind =
      | "REFRAME"
      | "REFUTE"
      | "REFUTE-IN-PRINCIPLE"
      | "CONCEDE-AND-DISCIPLINE";

    export interface Continuation {
      stance: "press-further" | "reject-premise" | "dismiss";
      label: string;
      text: string;
    }

    export interface Objection {
      id: string;                              // "14.1"
      title: string;
      urgency: 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 | 5;
      targetClaimIds: string[];
      objectionText: string;                   // authored paraphrase
    }

    export interface Response {
      id: string;                              // same as Objection.id
      kind: ResponseKind;
      responseText: string;                    // authored
      continuations: [Continuation, Continuation, Continuation];
      openInOntologyClaimId: string;           // primary cited claim
      openInDiamondCaseId?: string;            // if applicable
      finalStripTemplate: string;              // parameterized
      authorApproved: boolean;
    }

    export const OBJECTIONS: Objection[];
    export const RESPONSES: Record<string, Response>;

C3. Copy rules (verbatim where indicated).

Tile labels (verbatim):

    TILE A — OBJECTION
    TILE B — THE BOOK'S RESPONSE
    TILE C — WHAT WOULD YOU SAY NEXT?

Response-kind badges (verbatim, uppercase mono, colored):

    REFRAME                     --warn tint
    REFUTE                      --ok tint
    REFUTE-IN-PRINCIPLE         --ok-dim tint
    CONCEDE-AND-DISCIPLINE      --neutral tint

The kind badge is not a self-congratulation for the book when it
is a REFUTE and a concession when it is a CONCEDE; it is a
methodological tag. The color palette is chosen to NOT valorize
REFUTE over REFRAME; both are legitimate argumentative moves and
the user should see that.

Continuation stance microcopy (verbatim, in the radio labels):

    "Press further: [authored phrasing]"
    "Reject the premise: [authored phrasing]"
    "Say the book is playing with words: [authored phrasing]"

The third option is mandatory because it is the hostile-reader
voice; inclusion is an intellectual-honesty requirement. The book
must be asked, inside its own dojo, whether it is doing
philosophy or doing rhetoric, and the user must have that option
available.

Final strip template (verbatim; fill-in in brackets):

    "The book's response here is a [KIND]. If you still reject
     the response, the disagreement is likely at [CLAIM-ID],
     which you can open in the Ontology."

Addendum for REFRAMES specifically (verbatim):

    "The book does not expect to convert a reader who rejects
     [CLAIM-ID]. It asks only that the reader state an
     alternative account that does not reduce to [ALT]."

For REFRAMES, [ALT] is authored per objection:

    14.1 -> "coherence or mysticism"
    14.6 -> "a theory of evidence that does not reduce to an
            argument for how to interpret evidence"

C4. What the mode must not say.

  - "Gotcha" (never, ever).
  - "The book destroys this objection" (no).
  - "Easy answer" / "obviously".
  - "Critics claim..." (use "the objection is...").
  - Any of the words "debunk", "takedown", "slam", "owns".
  - "You might not understand that..." or any phrasing that
    implies the dissenting user is uninformed. Disagreement is
    first-class; the book's posture is to locate disagreement,
    not to dismiss it.

----------------------------------------------------------------------
PART F — IMPLEMENTER NOTES
----------------------------------------------------------------------

F1. This mode will be read by some users as where the book is
    weakest. That is CORRECT. The mode is designed to let a
    skeptical reader see the book's weaknesses explicitly. Do not
    add rhetorical buttressing that softens this effect.

F2. If authoring the response text strays from the Guide's
    Chapter 14 content, pull it back. The dojo's value depends
    on the responses being the book's actual positions, not
    a spokesperson's recast.

F3. The rank-by-weight affordance is intentionally quiet. It
    should not be a prominent CTA. It earns the user's use
    after they have read at least three objections; before then
    it is not shown.

F4. When a REFRAME response is rendered, the addendum saying
    "the book does not expect to convert a reader who rejects
    [CLAIM-ID]" is the single most important line in this mode.
    Do not dilute it. It is the book's own acknowledgment of the
    limits of rationalist dialectic, and the mode's
    intellectual honesty depends on its presence.

F5. There is a temptation to add an eighth objection along the
    lines of "the book is too long / too dense / pretentious."
    Do NOT. That objection is not about the argument, and
    including it would invite a dismissive mode of reading that
    the entire design opposes. If the author wants a "style"
    objection, it belongs in the dossier, not the dojo.


End of P04.
