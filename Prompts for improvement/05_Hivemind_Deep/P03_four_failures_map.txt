P03 — FOUR FAILURES MAP
========================

Prerequisites: P01 (chassis); P02 is not required but is
assumed to be the second completed mode when shipping in order.
This prompt delivers MODE 3 — FOUR FAILURES MAP.

The Four Failures Map is the firm's self-portrait inside the product.
It answers the fourth teaching question from the INDEX: why is the
firm behind Hivemind shaped the way it is? Guide I §2 names four
structural failures of the consulting industry (hiring, analysis,
accountability, theatrics). Guide I §5 and §6 describe the firm-
side composition and hiring procedure. Guide I §4 lists the Seven
Core Beliefs. The Four Failures Map ties all three together: for
each failure, it shows (a) the industry's behavior, (b) the
structural response inside The Nash Lab, and (c) the belief(s)
from the seven-belief statement that underwrite that response.


----------------------------------------------------------------------
PART A — WHY THIS MODE EXISTS
----------------------------------------------------------------------

A1. The firm is legible only through its rejections. Describing
    The Nash Lab by listing its teams ("we have a CS team, a
    research team, a compliance team, and an executive team")
    is correct but uninteresting. The pedagogically valuable move
    is to explain each team as a structural answer to a specific
    industry failure. That framing is load-bearing to Guide I §2
    and §5 and is how the firm describes itself internally.

A2. The Seven Core Beliefs (Guide I §4) are the firm's hiring
    filter ("if you disagree with any of these, it will be
    difficult for you to align yourself with the company's
    mission"). The mode surfaces them as a drawer attached to the
    whole map, not as an afterthought: the beliefs are what make
    the four responses coherent as a single posture.

A3. This mode is the only place in the HVM experience where the
    FIRM is the subject. Modes 1, 2, 4, 5 are about the PRODUCT.
    Keeping that distinction tight matters because Hivemind's
    honesty posture (Guide I §3) rests on the firm doing one
    narrow thing — curating the peer-reviewed KB — and not
    impersonating the product in its own marketing.


----------------------------------------------------------------------
PART B — THE FOUR FAILURES AND THE FOUR RESPONSES
----------------------------------------------------------------------

Every entry below is grounded verbatim or paraphrased from Guide I.
Where a field is marked VERBATIM, the authored copy in the fixture
must match the Guide's phrasing word-for-word; where it is marked
PARAPHRASE, the implementer may tighten for the panel but must not
change the meaning.

B1. FAILURE I — HIRING.

    INDUSTRY BEHAVIOR (VERBATIM from Guide I §2):
        "Selection based on network, nepotism, and conformity —
         not problem-solving ability."

    SPECIFIC MECHANISMS (PARAPHRASE):
        - "First piece of advice" to aspiring consultants: network
          for an internal referral.
        - Recruitment concentrated at a handful of prestigious
          schools whose admissions are not pure merit tests.
        - The result: reproduction of a social class, not a filter
          for analytic ability.

    STRUCTURAL RESPONSE (Guide I §6): BLIND EXAMS, NO RESUMES.
        - Hiring is split into three populations (consultants,
          academics, engineers); each solved by a different blind
          procedure.
        - Consultants: five-hour problem set (math, philosophy,
          case analysis) + strategy-game tournaments (Go, Hex,
          Backgammon, Othello, Chess) as low-variance measurement
          of competitive strategic reasoning under time pressure.
        - Academics: executive selection OR consultant-body vote
          OR director of academic hiring (executive-confirmed),
          criterion = contribution to the peer-reviewed KB.
        - Engineers: same exam format as consultants with different
          weights; split into core SWE and forward-deployed.
        - No credential filter before the exam. Applicants are not
          asked where they went to school.

    CORE BELIEFS ENGAGED:
        - Belief 4 (networking structure is inefficient, not
          conducive to aligned hiring).
        - Belief 5 ("play the man, not the game" is accurate
          but not something to be proud of).

    FIRM-SIDE ARTIFACT: no single team; the hiring procedure itself
    is the artifact. Referenced from the consultants / academics /
    engineers populations described in Guide I §5 and §6.

B2. FAILURE II — ANALYSIS.

    INDUSTRY BEHAVIOR (VERBATIM from Guide I §2):
        "Arbitrary teams run on vague intuition, leaving no
         transferable principles and fostering client dependency."

    SPECIFIC MECHANISMS (PARAPHRASE):
        - Recent-graduate teams deliver advice under their own
          unpredictable judgment.
        - Organizational heuristics (MECE, etc.) are "better
          described as theatrical than theoretical".
        - Nothing learned on one engagement is rigorously encoded
          and carried to the next.

    STRUCTURAL RESPONSE:
        - PEER-REVIEWED KNOWLEDGE BASE (Guide I §3, §5.2): the
          analytical content comes from a centralized, peer-
          reviewed strategic KB, not from whichever juniors were
          staffed.
        - ADVERSARIAL THEORY NETWORK (Guide II): multi-agent
          system, each agent carrying a different framework,
          critiquing and revising each other's conclusions before
          output.

    CORE BELIEFS ENGAGED:
        - Belief 1 (business problems reduce to a small set of
          underlying illnesses; KB compounding is valuable).
        - Belief 2 (AI is capable of meritocratic analysis under
          provided principles).
        - Belief 6 (the scientific method works; peer review is
          not decoration).

    FIRM-SIDE ARTIFACT: the STRATEGIC RESEARCH TEAM (Guide I §5.2).
        - Pure academics: develop and curate the peer-reviewed KB.
        - Forward-deployed academics: embedded with clients, one
          per client, as personal theory assistants. They observe
          which frameworks the client's problems stress and feed
          that back into the research agenda.
        - This team structurally replaces the "junior consultant
          on engagement" role.

B3. FAILURE III — ACCOUNTABILITY.

    INDUSTRY BEHAVIOR (VERBATIM from Guide I §2):
        "Mutual scapegoating between firms and CEOs turns
         consulting's actual value into blame deflection."

    SPECIFIC MECHANISMS (PARAPHRASE):
        - The consultant-client relationship is structurally win-
          lose: success is shared credit; failure becomes the
          firm blaming execution and the client blaming advice.
        - Many engagements are hired less for the recommendation
          than for political cover the brand name provides.

    STRUCTURAL RESPONSE:
        - CLIENT-INFRASTRUCTURE DEPLOYMENT (Guide I §7, §3):
          Hivemind runs natively on the client's servers; data
          stays with the client.
        - AUDIT TRAIL (Guide I §3, §8): every agent utterance,
          critique, and revision is logged into an immutable
          trail that survives the engagement.
        - EXPLICIT DISCLAIMER (Guide I §7, §8): the firm
          disclaims responsibility for the conclusions the client
          draws. Engagements include an upfront clause that
          Hivemind's purpose is to surface rigorous analysis, not
          to guarantee outcomes; failure modes become updates to
          the KB.
        - The KB-as-feedback loop: because the KB is centralized
          and peer-reviewed, "admit failure and update the
          methodology" has a well-defined referent in The Nash
          Lab that it does not in a traditional firm.

    CORE BELIEFS ENGAGED:
        - Belief 7 (honesty is the best policy).
        - Belief 3 (consulting's core offer is external problem-
          solving, and the industry has lost its way).

    FIRM-SIDE ARTIFACT: the COMPLIANCE TEAM (Guide I §5.3)
    plus the AUDIT TRAIL as a product-level artifact.
        - Compliance team tracks regulatory change and updates
          the practicality network's KB so that Hivemind never
          tells clients to do things that are illegal, fraudulent,
          or unworkable.
        - The audit trail itself is owned by the client, not the
          firm; this is the fiduciary backbone of the
          accountability claim.

B4. FAILURE IV — THEATRICS.

    INDUSTRY BEHAVIOR (VERBATIM from Guide I §2):
        "Managerial jargon feigns prestige while obscuring
         clarity, deepening dependency."

    SPECIFIC MECHANISMS (PARAPHRASE):
        - Dense, buzzword-laden deliverables produced under the
          implicit rule that impression is a component of value.
        - "The most visible failure and, arguably, the least
          fundamental — but it compounds the other three".

    STRUCTURAL RESPONSE:
        - DIRECT OUTPUT, NO SLIDE-DECK THEATER. Hivemind's
          deliverable is the clustered-recommendation set with
          preserved per-framework rationales (Guide II §3) and
          an immutable audit trail, not a PowerPoint with
          pyramidal arguments.
        - FORWARD-DEPLOYED ROLES as translators, not presenters:
          FDEs and FDAs observe client use and feed frictions
          back into product and research; they are not slide-deck
          producers.
        - Public commitment to plain language: the Hivemind
          product pitch and core-beliefs document are written
          without the managerial vocabulary the firm critiques.

    CORE BELIEFS ENGAGED:
        - Belief 7 (honesty is the best policy).
        - Belief 4 (networking structure is inefficient —
          theatrics is part of that networking performance).

    FIRM-SIDE ARTIFACT: the CS TEAM (Guide I §5.1).
        - Software engineers build and maintain Hivemind.
        - Forward-deployed engineers embed with clients, one per
          client, to observe how the product is actually used and
          convert frictions into engineering work.

B5. THE EXECUTIVE TEAM.

    Guide I §5.4 names a fourth team: the executive team, which
    oversees the other three and tunes the fundamental Hivemind
    workflow design where necessary. It is not paired one-to-one
    with a failure. The map surfaces it as a fifth card below the
    four failure cards, labelled "EXECUTIVE", with a short
    description matching Guide I §5.4 verbatim or near-verbatim:

        "Oversees the other three teams and tunes the fundamental
         Hivemind workflow design where necessary. Owns product
         strategy, firm-side calibration (e.g., revising the
         similarity threshold used in solution aggregation), and
         capital."

    The fifth calibration responsibility — prompt engineering —
    is described in Guide I §5 as "an open organizational
    question" and should be displayed as such on the executive
    card, not resolved into one arrangement.

PART C — WHAT THE USER SEES
----------------------------------------------------------------------

C1. Layout.

    WIDE (hostWidthPx >= 960):
        Four FailureCards arranged in a 2×2 grid, each with the
        failure label (I/II/III/IV), its verbatim one-liner, and
        a short "RESPONSE:" row naming the structural response
        in caps. A fifth compact EXECUTIVE card sits below the
        grid, full-width and shorter.
        Each card has an EXPAND affordance (chevron or +) that
        opens a ResponsePanel drawer.

    STANDARD (640-960):
        Four cards stacked in a 2×2 grid if there's room; else
        a single column. Same expansion behavior.

    NARROW (<640):
        Single column; cards are full-width; expansion inline
        below each card.

    Above the grid, a single-line header:
        "FOUR STRUCTURAL FAILURES, FOUR STRUCTURAL RESPONSES"

    Below the grid (and the EXECUTIVE card), a single-line link
    button:
        "OPEN THE SEVEN CORE BELIEFS"
    which opens a PrinciplesDrawer.

    Note on identifying refIds for product cross-references: the
    event ids referenced by productCrossRef.refId are stable
    TranscriptEvent.id values from the DEFAULT theater run
    (DEFAULT_THEATER_RUN_ID from P01 C2, i.e., "suff3-feas55-v1").
    The fixture author finds them by opening theaterScript.ts and
    selecting an event whose type matches the intended hint:
        Failure II   -> any event with type === "cluster_formed"
                        where contributingAgentIds.length >= 2
        Failure III  -> any event with type === "output" OR a
                        mid-transcript event whose metadata is
                        representative
        Failure IV   -> the event with type === "output"
    P06's assertCrossReferences verifies resolution at build time.

C2. FailureCard.

    Front face (collapsed):
        - Roman numeral + name (mono 20px + serif 18px).
        - VERBATIM one-liner from Guide I §2 (serif 13px).
        - "RESPONSE:" + short structural-response label (mono 11px
          uppercase, e.g., "BLIND EXAMS, NO RESUMES").
        - Expand chevron (aria-expanded).

    Expanded (ResponsePanel inline or modal drawer, see C3):
        - INDUSTRY BEHAVIOR section (VERBATIM one-liner, then
          SPECIFIC MECHANISMS list).
        - STRUCTURAL RESPONSE section (full response description,
          multi-paragraph).
        - FIRM TEAM / ARTIFACT section (named team + paragraph).
        - CORE BELIEFS ENGAGED section (list of belief numbers with
          a short inline quote; clicking a belief opens the
          PrinciplesDrawer to that belief).
        - A "WHERE IT SURFACES IN THE PRODUCT" footer: one-line
          cross-reference into another mode via openInMode. For
          example:
              Failure II -> openInMode("theater", "<event-id-of-
              a-cluster-formed-event>") with hint "see the Monitor
              cluster two theory rationales side-by-side".
              Failure III -> openInMode("audit", "<event-id>")
              with hint "see the immutable audit trail".
              Failure IV -> openInMode("theater", "<event-id-of-
              output>") with hint "the product's deliverable is
              the clustered output itself, not a slide deck".
              Failure I -> no product cross-reference; the card's
              cross-link opens the PrinciplesDrawer at beliefs 4
              and 5 instead.

C3. Expansion behavior.

    WIDE: the ResponsePanel opens as a right-side drawer that
    slides over the right half of the mode body. The 2×2 grid
    stays visible on the left so the user can switch between
    failures without closing the drawer.

    STANDARD and NARROW: the ResponsePanel opens INLINE beneath
    the card; clicking another card collapses the open one and
    opens the new one. Only one failure expanded at a time below
    WIDE.

    A close affordance (X button + ESC key) collapses.

C4. The EXECUTIVE card.

    Full-width, not a failure; labelled "TEAM — EXECUTIVE" with
    a short body (from B5). It does NOT have an expansion; the
    card is itself the full description. This asymmetry is
    intentional: the executive team is not a structural
    response, it is the firm's self-governance layer.

C5. The PrinciplesDrawer (Seven Core Beliefs).

    Opens as a modal drawer from the right at WIDE; full-screen
    at NARROW. Shows seven numbered entries, each with:
        - Belief number (mono, large).
        - VERBATIM belief text from Guide I §4.
        - One-line commentary noting which of the four responses
          it underwrites (e.g., belief 6 -> Response II).
        - A compact "LOAD-BEARING" chip on beliefs 1 and 6
          (Guide I §4 flags these two as load-bearing for the
          whole product thesis).

    The drawer header reads:
        "SEVEN CORE BELIEFS (THE NASH LAB)"

    With a caption (verbatim from Guide I §4):
        "If you disagree with any of these principles, it will
         be difficult for you to align yourself with the
         company's mission."

    A small footer note (paraphrase) acknowledges that beliefs 1
    and 6 are explicitly load-bearing for the product thesis
    (belief 1: business problems reduce to a small set of
    underlying causes; belief 6: the scientific method works).

C6. What the user controls.

    - Expand any failure card.
    - Click any core-belief cross-reference in a response panel
      to open the PrinciplesDrawer at that belief.
    - Click a product cross-reference to jump to another mode.
    - Open and close the PrinciplesDrawer directly from the
      bottom-of-map link.

C7. What the user CANNOT do.

    - Reorder the failures. I, II, III, IV is Guide I's order and
      the pedagogical order.
    - Edit the beliefs. The PrinciplesDrawer is read-only.
    - Add a sixth team card. Guide I §5 lists four teams and one
      open organizational question (prompt engineering); the map
      reflects that exactly.

PART G — IMPLEMENTER NOTES
----------------------------------------------------------------------

G1. DO NOT sanitize the failure one-liners. Guide I §2's language
    is deliberately sharp: "nepotism", "theatrical", "scapegoating",
    "managerial jargon feigns prestige". Softening these to
    "selection based on network effects" or "presentational
    ambiguity" would destroy the diagnostic weight. The VERBATIM
    constraint in E2 is ship-blocking.

G2. DO NOT add a fifth failure. The firm commits to four, and the
    whole architecture of responses hinges on four. Adding a
    fifth would misrepresent the thesis.

G3. The product cross-references are one-way from the map into
    other modes. The modes do not link back into the map by
    default. If a user jumps from Failure II into the theater
    and wants to return, the browser back behavior + the tab
    bar's FOUR FAILURES tab suffice. Do not build a "return to
    map" breadcrumb.

G4. DO NOT show the failures as gradations ("Failure IV is the
    least fundamental" etc.). Guide I §2 does note that
    theatrics is the least fundamental of the four, but that
    ordering commentary is internal reasoning; the map shows all
    four as coequal structural problems. The commentary can be
    included as a small serif italic line at the bottom of
    Failure IV's ResponsePanel, prefaced with "Guide I §2 notes
    that theatrics is 'arguably the least fundamental — but it
    compounds the other three.'"

G5. The EXECUTIVE card asymmetry is intentional. Do not "fix" it
    by making executive a fifth failure. The firm has four
    failures it is answering; the executive team exists because
    any firm has a governance layer. Treating governance as a
    structural answer to a diagnosed failure would overclaim.

G6. The PrinciplesDrawer caption ("If you disagree with any of
    these principles...") is harsh in the tonality Guide I
    deliberately adopts. Keep it. Softening to "we look for
    alignment with these values" is a prose-lint failure and a
    misrepresentation of the firm's hiring posture.

G7. Belief 6 ("the scientific method works") is the most
    philosophically load-bearing claim the firm makes. It is
    what makes the peer-review layer more than window-dressing.
    Do not bury it. The LOAD-BEARING chip is mandatory.
