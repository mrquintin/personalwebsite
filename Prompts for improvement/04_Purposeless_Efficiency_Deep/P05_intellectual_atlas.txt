P05 — INTELLECTUAL ATLAS (MODE 5 of 5)
=======================================

Prerequisites: P01 (chassis + Ontology), P02, P03, P04.

GOAL OF THIS MODE

The book is written into and against a large body of Western
thought. A reader who encounters the book without knowing which
thinkers it endorses, productively engages, rebukes, or
defends will read many individual arguments as floating. The
Guide's intellectual-context chapter gives the shape; this mode
makes it navigable.

The atlas is not a bibliography, not a syllabus, and not a
"recommended reading" list. It is a map of which thinkers the
book takes a stance on AND WHY. Each thinker card must answer
three questions in one place: (1) which specific claim(s) of
theirs the book engages; (2) what stance the book takes
(endorsed / productively engaged / rebuked / defended); (3)
which of the book's own claims rests on or responds to the
thinker's claim.

A secondary function of this mode: the glossary. The book has
~40 technical terms whose definitions are load-bearing (the
Guide appends a full index). A user who reads about, say,
"severance of dialogue" in a ClaimCard or a reveal text, needs
a one-click definition. The glossary strip in this mode is that
reference, shared across all modes via portal.


----------------------------------------------------------------------
PART A — THE FOUR STANCES
----------------------------------------------------------------------

Every thinker in the atlas is tagged with exactly one stance:

    ENDORSED
        The book agrees with the thinker on a load-bearing
        claim and builds on it. Examples from the Guide: Hayek,
        Rand, Adam Smith, Burke, Strauss, Hoppe, Thiel.

    ENGAGED (or "productively engaged")
        The book takes the thinker's claim seriously, often
        accepting a diagnosis while rejecting a prescription.
        Example from the Guide: Marx (silent compulsion
        accepted as diagnostic; prescription rejected),
        Durkheim (anomie accepted; remedy rejected), Rousseau,
        Hegel, Keynes.

    REBUKED
        The book argues specifically against the thinker's
        position. Example from the Guide: Fukuyama (end of
        history).

    DEFENDED
        The book defends the thinker against misreadings.
        Example from the Guide: Nietzsche (read as post-
        foundational rather than nihilistic).

There is no "neutral" category. A thinker who does not take one
of these four stances is not in the atlas. This is not an
encyclopedia; it is a stance map.


----------------------------------------------------------------------
PART B — WHAT THE USER DOES
----------------------------------------------------------------------

B1. Entry.

Mode opens on the AtlasOrbit: a radial layout with the book's
title at the center and four concentric arcs, one per stance.
Each arc holds thinker chips, arranged by rough alphabetical or
authored order. Arc colors reuse existing tokens:

    ENDORSED    outer arc       --ok tint
    ENGAGED     upper-right arc --warn tint
    REBUKED     lower-left arc  --err tint
    DEFENDED    inner arc       --accent tint

The orbit is NOT force-directed. Positions are authored
coordinates in thinkers.ts. This is the same pattern as the
ontology graph and for the same reason: a readable diagram
beats a procedurally generated one.

NARROW layout degrades the orbit to a list grouped by stance,
not a smaller orbit. A tiny orbit is illegible.

Header microcopy:

    "Thinkers the book takes a stance on. Click any name."

B2. The thinker drawer.

Clicking a thinker opens ThinkerDrawer. Contents (in strict
order):

    Header:
        Thinker name (serif, display).
        Stance badge (ENDORSED / ENGAGED / REBUKED / DEFENDED).
        Dates and one-line positioning ("Scottish moral
        philosopher, political economist, 1723-1790").

    Body:
        THE BOOK ENGAGES
            A 2-4 sentence authored statement of exactly which
            claim(s) of the thinker's the book engages.
            Specific. No "his philosophy generally".

        THE BOOK'S STANCE
            A 2-4 sentence authored statement of WHY the book
            takes the stance it does. For ENDORSED and REBUKED,
            this is the load-bearing disagreement or agreement.
            For ENGAGED, this is the acceptance-with-revision
            move. For DEFENDED, this is the misreading the book
            rejects.

        CONNECTED CLAIMS
            A bullet list of IDs from P01 (e.g., "CRI",
            "MCS") that cite this thinker, each a link that
            opens the claim in Ontology.

        COUNTER-RECOMMENDATION (OPTIONAL)
            A sentence of the form "A reader who rejects the
            book's stance here should look at [ALTERNATIVE
            THINKER]'s [WORK], which [WHAT IT ARGUES]."
            This is authored per thinker where it applies;
            omitted where it does not.

    Footer:
        CLOSE button (ESC).
        A small "WHERE ENCOUNTERED" strip showing all chapters
        in which the thinker appears, per the Guide. Example
        for Marx: "Ch.10, Ch.13, Ch.14.3".

B3. The glossary strip.

A persistent bottom strip, collapsed to 28px height by default,
showing a single line:

    "GLOSSARY | anomie | argumentative combat | base-horizon
     | coherence-reality isomorphism | ... (+36)"

Each term is a clickable chip. Clicking opens a small definition
popover anchored to the chip: name, 1-2 sentence authored
definition, optional claim refs into Ontology.

The strip's "+36" affordance opens the full glossary view (a
two-column list on STANDARD/WIDE, single column on NARROW) with
a search-within-glossary input.

This glossary strip is AVAILABLE IN ALL MODES, not only the
Atlas. It is instantiated once in the PrpExperience chassis
(P01) and rendered by portal across modes. It is documented
here because the Atlas mode is where its primary curation lives
and where the design review should verify it.

C4. v1 thinker list (minimum).

    ENDORSED:
        Hayek      — spontaneous order; knowledge problem;
                     signal-detection function of prices.
        Rand       — moral defense of the entrepreneur; the
                     creative-act reading of production.
        Adam Smith — division of labor; the market as
                     coordinator; the moral-sentiments reading
                     alongside Wealth of Nations.
        Burke      — the wisdom in inherited institutions; the
                     conservative caution against rationalist
                     reconstruction WITHOUT coherence.
        Strauss    — esoteric/exoteric distinction;
                     Straussian moment; the dangers of the
                     purely exoteric reading.
        Hoppe      — argumentation ethics; anarcho-capitalism
                     as the logical extension of argument-is-
                     the-only-legitimate-conflict.
        Thiel      — the entrepreneurial monopolist as
                     coherence-discoverer; the "secret" as
                     a pre-coherence insight.

    ENGAGED:
        Marx       — silent compulsion as diagnostic; means of
                     production as a discoverable constraint;
                     prescriptions rejected on MCS grounds.
        Durkheim   — anomie as a real phenomenon; collective
                     consciousness as a mislabeling of
                     something the book relabels.
        Rousseau   — general will critique; the book accepts
                     the problem and rejects the solution.
        Hegel      — dialectic as a formal pattern; the book
                     appropriates the pattern without the
                     historicist metaphysics.
        Keynes     — aggregate demand as a real short-run
                     phenomenon; the book engages without
                     endorsing the policy conclusions.

    REBUKED:
        Fukuyama   — end-of-history thesis rebuked on grounds
                     that coherence-testing has no termination
                     condition; civilization's argument is
                     not settled.

    DEFENDED:
        Nietzsche  — defended against the nihilist reading; the
                     book reads him as post-foundational, a
                     recognizer of the base, and a proponent of
                     faith-in-reason despite it.

----------------------------------------------------------------------
PART D — COPY RULES FOR THIS MODE
----------------------------------------------------------------------

D1. Stance badges (verbatim, uppercase mono).

    ENDORSED
    PRODUCTIVELY ENGAGED
    REBUKED
    DEFENDED

Rendered with existing color tokens. The full phrase
"PRODUCTIVELY ENGAGED" (not "ENGAGED" alone) is used in the
drawer header to avoid ambiguity; the chip label in the orbit
uses "ENGAGED" for brevity. This is a deliberate inconsistency;
keep it.

D2. Body section headers (verbatim, small serif, uppercase):

    THE BOOK ENGAGES
    THE BOOK'S STANCE
    CONNECTED CLAIMS
    COUNTER-RECOMMENDATION       (only when populated)
    WHERE ENCOUNTERED

D3. What the mode must not say.

  - "Influences" (too vague; use "engages" or "endorses").
  - "Critiques" (too polite for the rebukes; use "rebukes").
  - "Misunderstood" by itself (the book says the thinker is
    MISREAD BY OTHERS, not that the thinker is inherently
    hard to understand).
  - "[Thinker] said that..." (use "[Thinker] argues/argued/
    claims/claimed" — precision about verbal status matters
    here).
  - Any ranking language ("the greatest", "the most important").

D4. Proper nouns and naming.

Use the thinker's commonly cited surname for orbit chips (HAYEK,
RAND, SMITH). Full name in drawer header. For Nietzsche, use
"Nietzsche" (not "F. Nietzsche"). For Adam Smith specifically,
use "ADAM SMITH" on the chip (not "SMITH") to avoid confusion.

----------------------------------------------------------------------
PART G — IMPLEMENTER NOTES
----------------------------------------------------------------------

G1. Do NOT animate the orbit. "Orbital motion" is a design
    cliche that degrades readability, creates vestibular
    accessibility problems, and suggests a scientific precision
    the mode is not making. The orbit is a static diagram with
    coordinates.

G2. The orbit is visually rich; resist filling it with dozens
    of thinkers. Under 20 in v1 is preferable to 30+; legibility
    is the value.

G3. The COUNTER-RECOMMENDATION field is important and easy to
    skip. Its purpose is intellectual honesty: for every stance
    the book takes, a critic's canonical alternative should be
    named, if one exists. For the REBUKED of Fukuyama, for
    instance: "A reader who rejects the book's rebuke of
    Fukuyama should look at Fukuyama's own rejoinder essays,
    which — " etc. Author this per thinker; not all fourteen
    require it, but at least five should.

G4. The glossary and the ontology are different objects.
    Glossary terms define vocabulary; ontology claims are
    propositions. Do not merge them. A reader who wants the
    definition of "anomie" is not asking for a claim; a reader
    who wants "the book's view on anomie" needs the
    ontology path from the glossary via claim ref.

G5. If a thinker is tagged with more than one stance in draft
    (e.g., Marx is arguably REBUKED on prescription and
    ENGAGED on diagnosis), force a choice. Single-stance is
    the rule; the drawer body makes the nuance visible without
    multi-tagging. This is not oversimplification; it is the
    mode's diagnostic discipline.


End of P05.
