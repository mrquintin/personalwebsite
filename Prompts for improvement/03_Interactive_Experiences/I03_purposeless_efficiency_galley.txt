================================================================================
I03 — PURPOSELESS EFFICIENCY :: "THE GALLEY"
================================================================================

PURPOSE
-------
Build the in-accordion interactive experience for the Purposeless
Efficiency panel. The experience is a bespoke literary reading surface
— a "galley" — in which a visitor reads a short, curated passage from
the manuscript, accompanied by a running annotation margin, a visible
"argument spine" glyph that shows where the passage sits in the book's
five-pillar structure, and a small set of interactive affordances that
surface the argumentative substructure without spoiling the book.

This IS the primary body of the Purposeless Efficiency panel on both
"/" and /purposeless-efficiency.

THE INSTRUMENT
--------------
The name of this experience, displayed in the scaffold meta-strip:

    PURPOSELESS EFFICIENCY :: THE GALLEY · glry-v1.0

"Galley" is a printer's term for the first typeset proof of a book,
before binding, read by the author and editors with margins wide
enough to write in. It is the literal correct term for what this
surface is — an unbound proof of the first book in a five-volume
series, laid out so a careful reader can do what a careful reader does.

WHY THIS IS BESPOKE
-------------------
The Hivemind Receiver (I02) and the Theseus Reflection Lab (I04) are
both computational instruments dressed in terminal chrome. The Galley
cannot be that. A book does not gain meaning from a five-layer score;
it gains meaning from being read. The Galley is therefore the one
surface on the site where the user's action is "read slowly" and where
the site's job is to respect that. Its visual family-resemblance to
the other experiences comes from the ExperienceScaffold; everything
inside the scaffold body is intentionally a different world.

The Galley is the site's answer to the question: how do you demonstrate
an unpublished book without reducing it to a blurb? By letting a
visitor actually read one excerpt, annotated with the thinking a
careful editor would write in the margin, with a visible cue about how
the excerpt connects to the book's larger argument.

SOURCE MATERIAL
---------------
The five themes Michael named in the transcript:

    I   Corporatism
    II  Gamification of industry
    III Incumbency
    IV  Complacency
    V   Economic revolution

Guide-8 of the existing prompt suite (08_project_purposeless_efficiency.txt)
has already defined the book's visual surface — serif body, paper
background (oklch 0.20 0.01 75), drop caps, 64ch measure, small-caps
author byline. The Galley inherits every typographic decision from that
prompt. This prompt adds interactivity; it does not redesign the reading
surface.

THE INTERACTIVE STRUCTURE
-------------------------
The Galley is a single continuous vertical surface with three co-
present regions:

    ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
    │  SPINE (12%)   BODY (60%)          MARGIN (28%)       │
    │                                                        │
    │  I             ⎯⎯⎯ § ⎯⎯⎯                               │
    │  II            [drop cap + excerpt prose at 64ch]     │
    │  III *         .                                      │
    │  IV                                                   │
    │  V             .                                      │
    │                                                        │
    └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  SPINE  — a left rail listing the five pillars, one per row, in small
           caps. The pillar the current excerpt belongs to is marked
           with a dot and rendered in --fg-hi. The others remain in
           --fg-mute. Clicking a pillar swaps the active excerpt to
           one drawn from that pillar (or displays "coming soon" if
           that pillar has no excerpt yet). The spine itself never
           animates; the dot and color swap are instant.

  BODY   — the excerpt, serif, 64ch, drop cap on the opening
           paragraph. The body is ALSO the interactive surface:
           specific sentences are subtly underlined on hover, and
           clicking them pins an annotation in the margin. See
           "annotation dynamics" below.

  MARGIN — the running annotation column. At idle it displays:
             (a) the current pillar title and its one-sentence claim,
             (b) a tiny "argument spine glyph" (see below),
             (c) a small "editor's index" of two or three
                 pre-authored margin notes for the current excerpt,
                 displayed as clickable rows that scroll the body to
                 the anchored sentence and expand the note inline.
           When the visitor clicks a sentence in the body, the margin
           swaps to show the annotation anchored to that sentence,
           then fades back to the editor's index after a short delay
           unless the visitor pins it.

THE ARGUMENT-SPINE GLYPH
------------------------
The single small visualization the Galley earns, displayed at the top
of the margin column for every excerpt:

    ┌ ARGUMENT SPINE ─────────────────────────────┐
    │  I ─ II ─ III ─ IV ─ V                      │
    │  ·   ·   ●   ·   ·                          │
    │                                              │
    │  pillar · III · incumbency                  │
    │  position · ¶ 6 of 12 (§ 3.2)               │
    └──────────────────────────────────────────────┘

A horizontal sequence of five nodes, the active pillar filled, the
others muted. Below, two lines of metadata: which pillar the excerpt
belongs to and where in the book's structure it sits (chapter and
paragraph, if the excerpt has that annotation).

The glyph's purpose: to give a careful reader an immediate "you are
here" signal on a five-book structural map. It is not a toy; it is
architecture.

ANNOTATION DYNAMICS
-------------------
Three states for a sentence in the body:

  DEFAULT        normal serif, no underline
  HOVER          faint underline in --fg-mute, cursor is help
  PINNED         the sentence remains underlined in --fg-dim even on
                 blur, and the margin shows its annotation

Annotation content is a short operator-voice note, typically 40–120
words, formatted as monospace to contrast the serif body. Each note
has exactly one of three "kinds":

  CLAIM          A restatement of the sentence's load-bearing claim,
                 stripped of rhetoric.
  OBJECTION      A steel-manned counter-argument the visitor might
                 reasonably hold. Useful for priming a reader to
                 notice where the book is about to move.
  CONNECTION     A cross-reference to another pillar or another
                 excerpt, indicating that the argument recurs.

Annotations are authored by Michael (and, later, by his editors) into
a typed module; see DATA CONTRACT. There is no generative component.
A reader should come away with the sense that every note is in
Michael's voice and consistent with the book's thesis.

THE AFFORDANCE SET
------------------
The minimal set of interactive controls exposed by the Galley:

  [ read aloud ]        toggle a monospace pacing mode where each
                        sentence advances on space-bar; the current
                        sentence is at 120% leading, others dim;
                        intended for close reading, not TTS.
  [ reveal annotations ] toggles whether hover-underlines render at all;
                        the default on first mount is OFF so the
                        surface reads first as a book. Power users
                        turn it on.
  [ pin margin ]        pins the current margin annotation so clicks
                        elsewhere in the body don't displace it.
  [ next excerpt ]      rotates to the next excerpt (sorted first by
                        pillar, then by position in the book). A
                        keyboard shortcut [ → ] also advances; [ ← ]
                        goes back.
  [ copy citation ]     copies a BibTeX-style citation of the excerpt
                        to the clipboard, including the pillar and
                        paragraph index.
  [ support the book ]  opens a non-commercial "notify list" modal; if
                        no list exists, opens a mailto: per the spec
                        in Prompt 08 §8.

All controls sit in a small monospace toolbar flush to the top of the
body column — a horizontal run of labels, separated by hairlines, no
icons. The toolbar is the one piece of non-serif UI inside the Galley
body. Keep it restrained.

EXCERPT CATALOG
---------------
At launch, the Galley ships with one excerpt per pillar that HAS
finalized prose, and an honest "— excerpt coming soon —" placeholder
for pillars that don't yet. Each excerpt entry:

  - 300–1,000 words of serif prose (MDX or plain text).
  - 2–6 annotation anchors, each a {sentenceId, kind, body}.
  - The pillar metadata and the structural position ("§ 3.2, ¶ 6 of
    12") if known.
  - A one-line seed quote used on the landing teaser (referenced from
    Prompt 03 / Prompt 08).

Where Michael has not yet finalized content, authored placeholder
prose in his voice is acceptable and must be clearly marked with a
TODO(michael) comment inside the MDX module. Placeholders must NOT
ship on production branches; the Galley's build refuses to mount an
excerpt whose placeholder flag is true in NODE_ENV=production. The
Galley instead shows the "— coming soon —" frame for that pillar.

VISUAL LANGUAGE
---------------
Inherited from Prompt 08:

  - Background: oklch(0.20 0.01 75) — the "paper" tone
  - Body: Source Serif 4 at --t-md, measure 64ch, line-height 1.7,
    hyphens auto, hanging punctuation
  - Drop cap on the opening paragraph of every excerpt, floated
    left, scaled 3x, serif
  - Small-caps monospace for the author byline, pillar title, and
    section header
  - The annotation body is monospace, 12–13px, color --fg-dim
  - The Galley surface is the ONE place on the site where a serif
    typeface appears in the body. Do not leak it into the margin or
    the spine; those remain monospace.

Motion:

  - No parallax. No page-turn animation. The Galley does not pretend
    to be a paper book; it is a galley proof on a terminal.
  - Swapping excerpts crossfades the body (~160ms opacity tween) and
    snaps the spine dot to the new pillar with no tween.
  - Pinning an annotation fades it in over 120ms in the margin and
    renders a 1px hairline from the sentence to the margin rail,
    drawn in SVG in --fg-mute at 0.5 opacity. Removing the pin
    removes both with the inverse tween.
  - reducedMotion: every tween becomes 0ms; the pin hairline remains
    but without a drawing animation.

ADAPTATION TO hostWidthPx
-------------------------
  < 640 px    The margin collapses and annotations render as footnotes
              beneath each paragraph; the spine collapses to a single-
              line pillar crumb above the toolbar. "Read aloud" mode
              remains available.
  640–960 px  Two-column layout: body (70%) + margin (30%); spine
              collapses to an inline row beneath the toolbar.
  960–1280 px The full three-region layout as diagrammed.
  ≥ 1280 px   The full three-region layout, with the spine expanded
              to show each pillar's one-line claim under its label.

A11Y
----
  - The body excerpt is a single <article> with a readable aria-
    labelledby on its title.
  - Annotated sentences render as <button class="footnote-ref"> with
    aria-expanded reflecting pinned state.
  - When a sentence is pinned, the margin annotation is placed
    immediately after the sentence in the DOM via aria-describedby,
    so screen readers hear the body-margin coupling in order.
  - The argument-spine glyph has a text equivalent inside <details>
    summarising: "This excerpt belongs to Pillar III (Incumbency) at
    § 3.2, paragraph 6 of 12."
  - The "read aloud" mode, when toggled, announces "read-aloud mode on;
    space advances to the next sentence" via announce().
  - Every interactive element operable by keyboard; tab order runs
    toolbar → spine → body annotations → margin pins.

DATA CONTRACT
-------------
  type Pillar = {
    numeral: "I" | "II" | "III" | "IV" | "V";
    title: string;        // "Corporatism"
    claim: string;        // one sentence
  };

  type AnnotationKind = "CLAIM" | "OBJECTION" | "CONNECTION";

  type Annotation = {
    id: string;           // "an.corp.1.ch2.p3"
    sentenceId: string;   // matches a data-sentence-id attribute
    kind: AnnotationKind;
    body: string;         // 40–120 words, no markdown
  };

  type Excerpt = {
    id: string;                    // "ex.incumbency.structural_decay"
    pillarNumeral: Pillar["numeral"];
    position: { section: string; paragraph: number; of: number };
    title: string;
    body: string;                  // serif prose; plain text with
                                   // sentence-boundary markers
    annotations: Annotation[];
    placeholder: boolean;          // true if prose is not final
  };

ACCEPTANCE CRITERIA
-------------------
  - Opening the Purposeless Efficiency panel on "/" reveals the
    Galley; its first excerpt is displayed in full, with the
    argument-spine glyph pointing to the correct pillar.
  - Hovering a sentence (when annotations are enabled) underlines
    exactly that sentence. Clicking a sentence pins its margin note.
    Clicking elsewhere unpins unless "pin margin" is on.
  - The pillar spine swaps the active excerpt on click; swap runs
    under 200ms; the argument-spine glyph updates.
  - Keyboard: → and ← advance and retreat through excerpts; Space
    advances sentences in read-aloud mode; Tab cycles through the
    toolbar controls.
  - Placeholders in NODE_ENV=production render a "— excerpt coming
    soon —" card instead of the placeholder text.
  - prefers-reduced-motion disables the crossfade and the hairline
    draw; everything else remains functional.
  - No external UI libraries other than what was already required by
    prior prompts. No TTS. No generated content.

DO NOT
------
  - Do not add simulated "pages" or a page-turn animation. The Galley
    is a scrolled continuous surface; pretending otherwise is cheap.
  - Do not add a "buy now" button. The book is not published.
  - Do not add book reviews or blurbs. They do not exist yet.
  - Do not use the Galley to tease unwritten pillars by showing
    placeholder prose; use the "— coming soon —" frame. Honesty here
    protects the book.
  - Do not animate annotations as if they were speech bubbles. They
    are editorial notes; they render in place.
  - Do not add a "dark/light" toggle exclusive to the Galley. Site-
    wide theme applies; the paper background is a tone, not a mode.

STRETCH
-------
  - A "close reader" view, toggled via the toolbar, that renders the
    excerpt's sentences as a numbered list (mono, 13px), with their
    annotations hanging below each, one-screen-at-a-time, like a
    Loeb classical edition with facing notes. Optional; this is a
    gift for the operators-with-notebooks crowd.

  - A "cite-as" permalink per excerpt: the Galley exposes a URL of
    the form /purposeless-efficiency?ex=ex.incumbency.structural_decay
    that, when visited, boots the Galley directly into that excerpt.
    Useful for Michael sharing an excerpt over email or in a talk.

  - A "rebuild this paragraph" debug affordance in development that
    displays, beside each paragraph of the body, the count of
    annotation anchors on it and a warning if the sentence IDs in the
    prose don't match the IDs in the annotations module. A small aid
    for authorial housekeeping.
